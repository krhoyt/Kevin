## Orientation

I sometimes wonder if I picked my career, or if my career picked me.

Either way, my career has been largely driven by the excitement I get from putting a button on the screen. From Java AWT and Java Swing, to Flash and Flex, to web development, native smartphone development, and even IoT hardware. It does not matter much to me what the platform is - that API can be learned - but what you can do for the user when they click a button is limitless.

> AI/ML takes what you can do with a button on the screen to an unprecedented level of capability.

Second only to the button has been customer interaction. It does not matter what you have built if you have no customers. When you have a customer, you need to listen to them, and adapt your product accordingly - even if it takes you in a new and unexpected direction. I am adept at these conversations from technical sales to developer conference audiences, from product owners (both internal and external) to executives.

I especially enjoy communicating with developers about emerging technologies, and how they can use those innovations to unlock new business value.

I have spent nearly 20 years of my career in management. To that end, when I think about what success looks like, I think about making my direct reports heroes for what it is that they want to accomplish with their careers. When aligned, if I can grow my replacement while delivering value to the business, then I have succeeded both as a manager and as a leader.

**DeVry University - Kansas City, MO**  
B.S. Computer Information Systems (1998)  
*Magna Cum Laude*

I was a Cryptologic Linguist (Military Intelligence) in the United States Army from 1991 - 1995. As a challenge from one of my commanding officers, I built my very own 486 computer from the ground up. This was an MS-DOS system. Before long I started teaching myself to write programs in C to make this expensive little metal box do something interesting. 

What does a Cryptologic Linguist do? Well, the linguist part should be pretty obvious. I spent 6 months in Monterey, CA, learning Spanish. It seems like an odd assignment since September 11, 2001, but back in 1990, when I enlisted, we were only a few short years past the invasion of Panama. There was also plenty of instability throughout South America.

The "cryptographic" bit is a little harder to explain. The role was basically focused on intercepting, identifying, and exploiting foreign voice communications. Much of that communication was encoded in one way or another, so understanding cryptography was an important skill. Work which I performed from the ground, in the air on specially equipped Blackhawk helicopters, and even at sea on the USS Eisenhower.

> I eventually upgraded the hardware to run Windows 3.1.

As my time in the military was coming to an end (in 1995), I needed to decide what to do next with my life. One day a recruiter from DeVry Institute of Technology (eventually DeVry University) came by to talk about the programs they offered. One of these was Computer Information Systems (CIS). It was pitched as Computer Science, but with a curriculum tuned to what businesses were hiring for at the time. 

Lessons I learned while in the military include:

- Never stop learning
- There is no "I" in team
- Diversity adds value
- The importance of defining specific objectives

The tuition for DeVry was $36,000 for the three-year program. The G.I. Bill (veteran benefit to pay for continuing education) was also $36,000 at that time. Well, there we go! I subsequently enrolled in the program. After my service was complete, I packed my life into my car and moved to Kansas City, MO. I started the program in June of 1995.

### DPMA/AITP

DeVry hosted a student chapter of the Data Processing Management Association (DPMA) where those of us who were really nerdy got together once per week to further discuss technology. 

The Web was becoming a thing at the time and we wanted a website for our little chapter, so we used the meeting time to learn HTML development - a skill too futuristic in 1995 for the curriculum of the school. Other students who were not part of the DPMA chapter wanted to learn HTML also, so we convinced the dean to let us have a room on the weekend to do just that. One might say that we delivered the first HTML course offered by DeVry.

Java surfaced around this time as well. Also not offered as a course at DeVry in 1996. I taught myself Java in order to add interactive "applets" to our chapter website. By the time my senior year rolled around in 1998, I elected to implement the algorithms in my security and cryptography class using JavaScript (another language I had to teach myself because, as you may have guessed, it was not yet taught at DeVry). Java and the Web would go on to define most of my early career from 1998 through 2007.

If DeVry did not teach HTML, JavaScript, or Java, then what programming languages *did* we learn? COBOL, PowerBuilder, Visual Basic, and eventually C/C++ (which I already knew from my aforementioned time in the Army back in 1995). In fact, our particular AITP chapter was a COBOL powerhouse. We participated in several regional programming competitions with other student chapters from other schools - and regularly placed in the top three if not winning the category outright.

There was of course a healthy dose of classes on computer networking, SQL and DB2, and normalization, finance and accounting, MS Office, and much more - most of which have served me well during my career. In June 1998, I graduated Magna Cum Laude with a Bachelor of Science in Computer Information Systems (CIS).

> In 1996, the Data Processing Management Association changed its name to the Association of Information Technology Professionals (AITP).

### Grapevine

**Webmaster**  
*1997*

My first job in technology was in 1997, at an Internet Service Provider (ISP) called Grapevine, which served the Kansas City area. Back then you had to dial into the Internet, and Grapevine was a local call center for that connection. You would pay Grapevine a monthly fee, and in turn they would allow your computer modem to dial into their system and connect to the Internet.

Grapevine also offered website development and hosting, and that is where I came in. My job was to administer Apache web servers and develop websites for customers. When sites needed data interaction, I used Common Gateway Interface (CGI) with Perl scripts to accept contact forms, send email, perform math for online calculators, authentication for sites with sensitive data, and whatever else came up.

I learned a lot about networking, web servers, what we would now call middleware (the back-end), and of course HTML, CSS, and JavaScript (the front-end). I was effectively delivering full-stack services in 1997. As the Internet gained popularity, Grapevine's market was eaten by large corporations and in June 1997, I needed to find a new job.

### Jack Henry & Associates (JKHY)

**Programmer**  
*June 1997 - June 1999*

Jack Henry & Associates still exists today as a publicly traded company. At the time, Jack Henry provided IT and infrastructure services for banks that were too small to staff their own. An example service would be paper check processing - scanning checks that their customers had written, and putting the data into the bank's system of record where it could be further processed for the corresponding value.

In June 1997, many of these small banks wanted websites, so Jack Henry put together an online services department. My job was to work with the banks to determine the information architecture of their sites, as well as working with in-house designers to develop a look and feel in line with the bank's brand. From there I would develop and deploy the websites using HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and even Java applets on occasion.

Many of the banks wanted "calculators" on their websites. An example of this might be a mortgage amortization schedule. Or a calculator that determined how much house or automobile you could afford. We selected a middleware technology called ColdFusion, by a company called Allaire, to build these calculators. 

ColdFusion had a markup-based syntax. For example, an `if` statement would be `<cfif a EQ b> ... </cfif>`. Where ColdFusion really shined was in the complex additional functionality wrapped into those tags. If you wanted to send an email, you could use `<cfmail>`. If you wanted to query a database, you could use `<cfquery> _SOME_SQL_ </cfquery>`. If you wanted to extend the markup with your own tags, you could do that through a C++ API. And that is exactly what we did.

Eventually, Jack Henry customers wanted online banking. We take online banking for granted these days, but that was breaking new ground back in the late 1990s. In our case, we needed to connect from ColdFusion to AS/400 systems via socket, and then issue the corresponding commands to make banking requests. This was accomplished through a series of custom C++ extensions that were developed for the ColdFusion middleware - think `<cfx_banking>`.

When I graduated from DeVry in 1998, I went full-time at Jack Henry. When I wanted to move to Denver, CO in 1998, they allowed me to work from the Denver, CO office (remote from the core team). I will always fondly remember Friday afternoons playing Monster Truck Madness online with the rest of the team.

I spent a few months in a closet with a rack of servers, working on a ColdFusion application for a shipping company (think bill of lading, etc.). There were only a few companies pushing forward emerging specifications for technologies like Java Servlets and Java Server Pages (JSP). One such company was Live Software, which made a (Java on the server) product called JRun. JRun was the first commercial servlet and JSP server, and we used it as the middleware for the search engine consulting project.

I came into Whittman-Hart full of early career confidence. I felt unstoppable. In an office full of highly specialized consultants, working on a wide diversity of projects, I learned that there are people who are better at some aspects of technology than I am - to know my limits and to defer to others when those limits are exceeded.

In 2000, Allaire, makers of the ColdFusion middleware product I had used extensively, called to see if I wanted a job on their sales team. Even though I had only been working as a consultant for a year, it was a dream job to work for the company whose product had largely defined my career up to that point.

I spent Fall 2000 traveling around from city to city, giving half-day presentations about ColdFusion to hotel conference rooms full of developers. I would fly out Monday, deliver a workshop on Tuesday AM, fly to the next city Tuesday PM, deliver a workshop Wednesday AM, fly to the next city Wednesday PM, repeat for Thursday, and fly home Friday AM. After he finished, I closed my laptop, turned off the projector, and told him that I would love to be educated about his business. > In 2026, the idea of embedding consultants at large customers to float around and inject product into projects has become a role unto itself called the Forward Deployed Engineer.

I even remember having a meeting at a large footwear manufacturer in Portland, OR, when an earthquake hit Seattle, WA. The entire office campus was literally bouncing around in the middle of the meeting - which was promptly rescheduled.

### Macromedia

**Principal Solutions Engineer**   
*June 2001 - December 2005*

Macromedia is probably most well-known for a product called, Flash. In the early 2000s, Flash was everywhere and growing. In 2001, Flash did not have robust networking support, but it did have the ability to load external Flash assets. In order to support customers who wanted more data-centric Flash content, Macromedia had introduced a product called Generator. Generator allowed content creators to embed placeholders in Flash content. When such an asset was requested, Generator would load external data and populate the placeholders. Generator was built on JRun.

Also around this time, Java on the server had matured into a series of specifications called Java EE.

Also around this time, Macromedia began the migration of ColdFusion from a C/C++ codebase, to a product that sat on top of a Java EE server.

All of this meant that I was busier than ever. Some days I was helping close the technical sale of JRun. Other days I was supporting a line-up of ColdFusion sales calls. 

As the Java version of ColdFusion got closer to launch, I started visiting user groups and meetups. Sometimes the group was interested in the innovations. More often than not, there were understandably concerned with migration and performance. My job was to have answers. To ensure customers stayed on the platform. And while the customer base did shrink over the following 20 years, there are still ColdFusion customers in 2026.

> In 2001, Macromedia held their first developer-oriented conference called, DevCon at the Walt Disney Swan and Dolphin in Orlando, FL. I got to present a handful of sessions. This is where I really got the conference bug, and have since gone on to present at well over 100 conferences around the globe.

In late 2003, I was approached by the product manager of a product with the code name of "Royale". She wanted me to be the lead sales engineer for the product when it was released, and gave me early access. Royale was released in March 2004 as Macromedia Flex - a markup-based approach to building Flash content. Flex used ActionScript 2.0 as the supporting language (effectively JavaScript), and was designed for creating data-oriented enterprise applications.

Flex was a complex sales cycle, taking 6 months or more to close. It initially sold for $15k/CPU, and ran on top of Java EE application servers. The average Flash developer would tell you that was a ridiculous price, and that they would never buy it. And while that may have been true for Flash developers, Java developers (who were more open to paying for application server software) loved it. I earned a trip to the sales club in 2004 as sales engineer of the year.

Another big seller for me in 2004 - 2005 was a Learning Management System (LMS) released by Macromedia called Breeze. Because of the level of interactivity that could be obtained with Flash content, it was very popular in the educational space - specifically corporations. I never thought that as a Java application server guy that I would be selling an LMS, but I had no problem picking up learning standards such as SCORM and AICC and talking to knowledge workers.

Those Breeze sales calls were really fun. They were a very different sales process from Java application developers. Breeze sales calls were often very creative, and with customers who were not programmers at all. I remember one particular customer meeting in a logging mill in Oregon - they were developing learning content for the mill operators. Big "danger" buttons and the likes.

At Macromedia I learned how to manage the large, complex, enterprise sales process. I also got re-introduced to my creative side; and that creative side was about to take off.

Macromedia was acquired by Adobe in December 2005.

### Adobe (ADBE)

**Senior Product Specialist**   
*December 2005 - April 2007*

In June 2006, Adobe Flex 2 was released with one monumental change - it no longer required a Java EE application server, and was now simply a Flash compiler. It also included robust data connectivity in the form of add-on products called Blaze and Data Services. 

A demonstration I loved to show was how fast a Flex/Flash application could load database data and page it in a data grid. You could make the data grid editable, and map the data to Java objects and database records - effectively a real-time object relational model (ORM) for Flash/Flex applications. You could go from "Hello World" to full CRUD in the space of an hour demonstration. 

As a compiler, Flex was very popular, and I spent many weekends at user group and conference events.

The nature of my role, while still sales, started to shift to supporting business development by working with vendors looking to include Macromedia technology into their own (often called "white label"). I would work closely with product teams from Macromedia and third parties to build out initial integration and proof of concept applications to help close the technical sale.

Another big shift was happening in the industry - the rise of the smartphone. No, the iPhone was still a little ways out. Today we would called these phones "feature phones". They had a screen, and some compute. Often a camera. But they were a far stretch from the iOS and/or Android phones that are commonplace in 2026.

These phone vendors were looking for a consistent way to present interactive content on their devices, and Macromedia answered with Flash Lite. In 2006 I was developing applications for smartphones using Flash. This even extended into set-top boxes for cable companies, and consumer electronics. This was all business development work that consisted mostly of working with the vendors, and occasionally helping content creators build applications that would feature on these devices (an early form of the commonplace application store we know from smartphones in 2026).

> I presented a session on the future of video delivery at a cable industry convention in 2006. I stood there and told this room full of hundreds of leaders from across the cable industry, that streaming would be the future. They almost laughed me off stage. Not even a year later, in 2007, Netflix started streaming.

**Platform Evangelist**   
*April 2007 - December 2008*

In 2007, Adobe was gearing up to release the Adobe Integrated Runtime, AIR. Adobe AIR provided a way to use Flash/Flex, as well as web standards, to build desktop applications. AIR had a captive WebKit instance, and the team was looking for an evangelist for the web standards side of the product. I approached the product team about being that person, and in April 2007 started a new role (more product than sales) as a Platform Evangelist.

My role was primarily to promote the use of web standards. In a company built on the legacy of Flash, I had my work cut out for me. I spent just as much time talking to product teams inside Adobe as I did to web developers outside the company. Then in June 2007, Apple released the iPhone and my job became a lot easier. The web browser was core to iPhone (and soon Android), where Flash was nowhere to be seen. If you wanted your content to be visible on smartphones, you wanted to build with web standards.

> Adobe did make an honest go of Flash for smartphones, but Steve Jobs personally refused to allow it.

In August 2007, Adobe rented a bus (a sleeper like politicians and musicians use), branded it for AIR, and sent ten employees, myself included, on the Adobe AIR Tour. The tour was three two-week trips around the United States, stopping nearly every other day in a new city to spend the entire day teaching developers how to build desktop applications with Flash, Flex, and web standards.

A slightly scaled back version was held in 2008, using trains in place of the bus, to traverse Europe, throughout which I also represented web standards.

Along the way, I started presenting at web developer conferences to include the very first jQuery Camp in October 2007.

At this point I had extensive developer experience, extensive sales experience, and extensive marketing experience, and was at the forefront of web standards. It was time to expand my role, and in February 2008, I was promoted to Manager of Technology Evangelism.

**Group Manager, Technology Evangelism**   
*February 2008 - November 2012*

My team started out as 3 people. It quickly grew to 5 people. Then it grew to 5 more in Romania. Then it grew 3 more in India. The senior person on the Romania team acted as the de facto country manager. For the India team, there was an assigned country manager. It is in this arrangement that I got my first taste of managing international teams, and the special requirements that come with them. These country managers were responsible for local needs (usually HR-related like compensation and other benefits), while I was responsible for managing the day-to-day work, professional growth, and overall direction of the teams.

It is during this time that I really start to formalize my management techniques such as: 

- Treating my direct reports like people, not cogs. 
- Trying to make them heroes for what it is that they want to achieve. 
- Facilitate serendipity to move the team (and company) forward. 
- Avoid asking people to do work that I would not do myself. 

I also learned that, at the time, I was not very good at "managing up". That is, making sure senior management saw the merits and wins of my team. This is a skill I would spend a considerable number of years developing, and it is probably still my weakest management skill.

My management style is very hands-on. I work best when I can manage, and deliver as an individual contributor. While I had an extensive team, I continued to grow my web development skills. In 2008, O'Reilly published Adobe AIR for JavaScript Developers, on which I was an author.

> Adobe has a great time off policy - they take the week of July 4 off, and they take the last two weeks of the year off. They also offer a sabbatical after five years. I learned to fly during my sabbatical. I am a certified private pilot. I fly small single-engine aircraft like the Cessna 172.

Despite all the drama, Flash did eventually make its way to Android in 2009. This brought with it a short resurgence in interest around Flash. For a while, on top of my web standards work, I also helped with the Android launch, and the launch of a Flash-oriented real-time messaging service called Adobe Flash Collaboration Services (AFCS) in 2009.

I will never forget the person on my US team who, in 2010, wrote a blog post telling off Steve Jobs over Flash. It was picked up by the New York Times. Management was furious. I immediately flew out to where the employee lived and we spent the weekend talking through the options to move forward. He took the path that allowed him to remain an employee, and still works for Adobe in 2026.

Back in 2008, on the Adobe AIR Tour, one of the web standards champions that was involved was a company called Nitobi, out of Vancouver, Canada. Nitobi was predominantly a software consulting company. They had created an HTML data grid that gave the Flex data grid a run for its money. They also created an open source mobile framework called, PhoneGap, which was released as 1.0 in 2011.

PhoneGap allowed web developers to build and deploy mobile applications for iOS and Android using pretty much the same code base. This was accomplished through a captive browser instance and a layer of JavaScript APIs that could be used to invoke native mobile systems. This was of real value to Adobe in the wake of the Flash/iOS situation, and I was asked to make introductions between Nitobi ownership and Adobe executive leadership. Later in 2011, Adobe acquired Nitobi and gave PhoneGap to open source as Apache Cordova, which still lives on in 2026.

Throughout the years of a rapidly maturing Web, I had developed a strong interest in IoT, hardware, and electronics. There was a sweet spot where amazingly creative people would leverage the commodification of hardware to build incredibly creative interactive art. These people became known as "makers" and that is where I turned next.

**Principal Evangelist**   
*November 2012 - November 2013*

As a Principal Evangelist I had the freedom to try new ideas, so I spun off hard (for better or for worse) into the emerging maker community. I developed relationships with manufacturers of laser cutters, CNC machines, and the emerging 3D printer. My goal was to get them to default to using Adobe creative products for input to their machines.

## People

### Frustrations

In a work setting, one of the the things I find frustrating is when people are not looking out for one another. As an individual contributor, I expect my manager to have my back. As a line manager, I expect the same from my manager. As a peer, I want to know from colleagues if I am not delivering appropriately; before something escalates. 

This behavior is right there in the lower levels of Maslow's hierarchy of needs - safety needs. Without safety needs being met, belonging does not develop. Team cohesion does not develop. Innovation does not develop because nobody feels safe enough to experiment.

### Trust

The quickest way to earn my trust is through humility. Not necessarily to me, but just the simple act of being humble. Somebody who says "I don't know". Somebody, regardless of their experience level, who can admit that there is something they do not know, and ask for help. As an individual contributor, and as a manager, I will always extend a hand to those that ask for help.

Conversely, somebody who uses the word "just" a lot raises alarm bells for me. As developers, we should all know that the devil is in the details. There is rarely a time when all that is needed is "just" to do a specific action. This term belies the accumulated knowledge that makes this task "simple". The term "just" suggest inexperience or a professional naivete. Where the term "just" really aggravates me is that it implicitly looks down upon somebody who does not have that knowledge - to whom the task would legitamately be difficult. When "just" is used in this setting it can make others feel less than, and I will not stand for it.

Developers deal in worlds of `if ... then` all day long. They have exceedingly sensitive bullshit meters. If somebody is saying that they can deliver out of arrogance, it will show. Do not try and impress me with how much you know - rather, actually know what you tell me you know. Deliver. That is the only metric that really matters.

### Disagreement

Amazon has a Leadership Principle that I think works well in most cases.

> **Disagree and commit**  
Leaders are obligated to respectfully challenge decisions when they disagree, even when doing so is uncomfortable or exhausting. Leaders have conviction and are tenacious. They do not compromise for the sake of social cohesion. Once a decision is determined, they commit wholly.

Breaking this down, let us say that we are planning a new feature, and that another person on the team disagrees with the priority of the feature. They should say so. As an IC, I want to hear others input. As a manager, not only do I want to hear more input, I want to be very sure that we avoid problems if at all possible. I encourage disagreement, and as a manager I work to make sure everybody feels comfortable enough to disagree.

At some point, a decision will be made. We all need to respect that decision and move forward. Holding onto grudges and gossip complaining in the back halls of the office is not going to change the decision, and not going to assist in the delivery. It will in fact behave like sandpaper, slowly wearing away the connective tissue of the team.

From a manager's perspective, I sometimes talk about how I do not need to be your friend. If you disagree with me, then that it fine. All I need to be to you as an IC is a focused point on the horizon - that is where we are going, what I am driving towards on the behalf of the company. If you disagree, then say so. Once a decision has been made however, turn back towards the horizon and focus on the objective.

### Feedback

I personally love feedback, both as an individual contributor, and as a manager. Good or bad, I want to know what people are thinking of me, of my performance, of the code I write, of my management skills. How can I improve if I do not have an objective appraisal of my skill? Those around me can expect the same level of feedback in return.

Last time I checked, CEOs are human beings just like me. They get up in the morning and put their pants on one leg at a time, just like everybody else. I am not intimidated by title or social standing, and will openly give feedback to anybody who wants it.

There is the point where feedback matters - who wants it. Not everybody does, and outside of performance reviews, that is fine. Certainly not everybody wants to received feedback in front of others, so discretion can be the better part of valor. This is where experience matters. It takes experience to know when and how to deliver feedback. Maybe feedback in the moment of a team meeting is best - to nip an issue in the bud. Maybe a team meeting it is not the best place for feedback. Perhaps a better alternative is to address the feedback in private.

The term "feedback" also tends to carry a negative connotation. I think space should be made for regular positive feedback. As a manager, I make it a point to heap out positive feedback (praise) wherever I can - it is free after all. Life is hard. You never know what somebody is going through. If you can brighten their day with just a little positive feedback, then why would you not?

From time to time, I have also been known to create custom awards to be handed out on a quarterly or yearly basis. This is a holdover from when I was in sales. It was common in my day for physical trophies to be handed out to those who hit certain milestones. 

I got a physical Worldwide Sales Engineer of the Year award in 2005, and a few others in a similar vien along the way. It has been more than 20 years, but I still know where that award sits in my house. I may not look at it as often as I once did, but when I do look at it, I feel a sense of pride and accomplishment - event after all this time. That feeling inspires me to want to hand out awards to my team members for their accomplishments.

### [Real-Time Drone Telemetry](https://kevinhoyt.com/blog/2015/05/14/drone-telemetry/)
*May 2015*

A real-time telemetry demonstration using a DJI Phantom 2 quadcopter equipped with an Arduino sensor array (GPS, compass, accelerometer, temperature, humidity) that transmitted flight data via XBee radio to an Inmarsat satellite modem. The data was then published through Kaazing Gateway using WebSockets, achieving less than one second latency from sensor to mobile device. This project explored the feasibility of a "black box in the cloud" for aircraft flight data recording, demonstrating how real-time publish-subscribe architecture could enable multiple stakeholders to independently record and process telemetry data.

### [Real-Time Engine Telemetry](https://kevinhoyt.com/2015/05/12/vehicle-engine-telemetry/)
*May 2015*

A project demonstrating real-time vehicle telemetry using an ELM-327 Bluetooth OBD adapter connected to a car's on-board diagnostics system. A Java program running on a laptop read engine data (RPM, speed, fuel level, coolant temperature) via serial communication, merged it with GPS coordinates from a USB module, and published the combined data through Kaazing Gateway using AMQP over WebSocket. The web client displayed the real-time telemetry on Google Maps with SVG-rendered gauges, all achieved for about $100 in hardware costs.
