1. Identity & Orientation (Ground Truth)

“Who is this person, and how should I interpret everything else?”

1.1 Professional Identity

One-paragraph synthesis of how you describe your career

Explicitly name your archetype(s): builder, educator, systems thinker, etc.

What you’re not trying to be seen as

Why it matters
This anchors tone and prevents contradictory summaries.

1.2 Career Through-Lines (Meta-Patterns)

Repeating roles you end up in

Types of problems you keep solving

Why certain domains recur (DX, docs, tooling, enablement)

Used to answer

“What defines Kevin’s career?”
“What kind of problems does he naturally gravitate toward?”

2. Experience & Evidence (STAR-Compatible Core)

“What has this person actually done?”

2.1 Role Index

For each role:

Context (company, era, constraints)

Mission as you understood it

What success actually meant (not the job description)

2.2 STAR Narratives (Atomic Units)

Each bullet becomes:

Situation

Task

Action

Result

Reflection ← critical addition

Reflection answers:

What you learned

What you’d do differently now

What this says about how you work

This is where personality quietly leaks in.

3. Skills & Capability Depth (How, Not Just What)

“What does ‘experienced’ actually mean here?”

This section is hugely valuable and rarely done well.

3.1 Skill Index (Canonical List)

Each skill gets its own node:

JavaScript

Svelte

Web Components

Python

DevRel

Documentation

Leadership

etc.

3.2 Per-Skill Deep Dive Schema

For each skill, include:

How I learned it

When

Why

Under what constraints

How I’ve used it

Production vs experimentation

Solo vs team

Scale and longevity

What I’m strong at

What I avoid or delegate

How I evaluate my level

Beginner / Proficient / Advanced / Expert (define these!)

Linked evidence

Blog posts

Code

Talks

Docs

Demos

Used to answer

“How deep is Kevin’s JavaScript experience?”
“Has he used Svelte in production or just demos?”

4. How I Work (Behavioral & Interpersonal)

“What is it like to work with this person?”

This is where recruiters actually get personality.

4.1 Decision-Making Style

How you approach ambiguity

Speed vs rigor tradeoffs

When you stop gathering information

4.2 Collaboration Style

Separate subsections:

With managers

With peers

With reports

With cross-functional partners

Include:

What earns your trust

What frustrates you

How you handle disagreement

4.3 Feedback & Conflict

How you give feedback

How you receive it

What you do when there’s tension

4.4 Boundaries & Non-Negotiables

Work patterns you won’t repeat

Cultural red flags

Conditions where you do your best work

Used to answer

“Would Kevin be a good fit for a fast-moving startup?”
“How does he handle conflict?”

5. Growth, Change, and Self-Awareness

“Is this person reflective and adaptable?”

5.1 Changed Beliefs

Things you believed earlier that you don’t anymore

Why those beliefs changed

5.2 Mistakes & Regrets

Not performative failure

Real judgment calls you’d revise

5.3 Learning & Staying Current

How you assess new tech

How you avoid hype

How you ramp into unfamiliar domains

6. Practical Hiring Logistics (Explicit Answers)

“Answer the questions recruiters always ask.”

Make these explicit, boring, and canonical so the AI never guesses.

6.1 Salary Expectations

Range

What affects flexibility

What matters more than raw number (if applicable)

6.2 Availability & Start Date

Earliest start

Constraints

Willingness to overlap/consult

6.3 Role Preferences

Roles you’re actively seeking

Roles you’d consider

Roles you’re not interested in

7. Semi-Personal / Human Context (Safe Personality)

“Make the answers feel human without crossing boundaries.”

This is smart—and safe—if scoped correctly.

7.1 Light Personal Preferences

Favorite foods

Colors

Places

Cat/dog/both

Coffee/tea

Introvert/extrovert tendencies (behavioral, not identity)

Rules

Preference, not identity

No health, politics, religion, etc.

Frame as “often” or “tends to,” not absolutes

7.2 Outside-of-Work Interests (Optional)

Only include things that:

Influence how you think

Tie back to curiosity, learning, or creativity

8. Writing, Thinking, and Public Artifacts

“Show, don’t tell.”

8.1 Blog Index

For each post:

One-paragraph summary

Why you wrote it

What it demonstrates (skill, value, judgment)

Relevant skills/topics

8.2 Canonical Examples

Explicitly mark:

“Best example of technical depth”

“Best example of leadership thinking”

“Best example of teaching”

This allows answers like:

“Here’s a post where Kevin explains exactly how he approaches X.”

9. How to Use This Knowledge Base (Instructions)

“Tell the AI how to behave.”

Include:

What this document is optimized for

What it should not be used to infer

Tone preferences for answers

When to cite examples vs summarize
